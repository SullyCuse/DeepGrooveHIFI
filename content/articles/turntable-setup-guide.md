A turntable done right is one of the most satisfying audio experiences available. A turntable done wrong — with a misaligned cartridge, incorrect tracking force, or an unlevel platter — sounds veiled, thin, and groove-damaging. Setup matters more than almost any other variable in vinyl playback. This guide walks through every step.

## What You'll Need Before You Start

Before touching the turntable, gather these tools:
- A bubble level (a small torpedo level from a hardware store works perfectly)
- A stylus force gauge (a cheap digital scale accurate to 0.01g)
- A cartridge alignment protractor (free paper versions are downloadable online; the Baerwald or Stevenson alignments are most common)
- A record cleaning brush or carbon fiber brush
- A test record (optional but helpful — the Hi-Fi News Analogue Test LP is the industry standard)

## Step 1: Choose a Stable, Level Surface

Place your turntable on a surface that is:
- **Level:** Use your bubble level and adjust the turntable's feet until the platter surface is perfectly level in both axes. Even a slight tilt causes the stylus to apply uneven force to both groove walls, reducing channel balance.
- **Rigid:** Avoid surfaces that vibrate or resonate. Turntables are sensitive to physical vibration — footfalls, speaker output, HVAC — that causes unwanted tonearm movement.
- **Away from speakers:** Speaker vibrations travel through furniture and floors into the turntable. Dedicated turntable stands, wall-mounted shelves, or isolating platforms mitigate this.

## Step 2: Install or Verify the Cartridge

If your turntable came with a cartridge pre-installed, proceed to alignment (Step 3). If you're installing a new cartridge:

Mount the cartridge body to the headshell using the provided screws. Finger-tighten them — you'll need to make fine alignment adjustments before tightening fully. Connect the four color-coded tonearm wires to the cartridge pins: typically white (left positive), blue (left negative), red (right positive), and green (right negative). Consult your cartridge manual to confirm pin assignments.

## Step 3: Align the Cartridge

Cartridge alignment determines the angle at which the stylus sits in the groove at different points across the record. Poor alignment increases tracking distortion — a fuzzy, smeared quality on inner grooves in particular.

**Using a protractor:**
1. Place the alignment protractor flat on the platter
2. Move the tonearm so the stylus tip sits over the first alignment point (the protractor will have two points marked)
3. Rotate the cartridge in the headshell until the cartridge body is parallel to the protractor grid lines
4. Move to the second alignment point and verify — adjust if needed
5. Alternate between the two points until the cartridge is parallel at both
6. Tighten the headshell screws firmly once aligned

This process can take 15–30 minutes for beginners. It's worth the time.

## Step 4: Set Tracking Force

Tracking force is the downward pressure the stylus exerts on the record. Too light, and the stylus skips and distorts. Too heavy, and it increases groove wear without improving tracking. Both extremes damage records.

**How to set tracking force:**

1. Zero-balance the tonearm: with the anti-skate set to zero, remove the stylus guard, and adjust the counterweight until the tonearm floats horizontally with no tendency to rise or fall.

2. Set the tracking force dial (on the counterweight) to zero.

3. Turn the counterweight (not the dial) toward the rear of the tonearm until your stylus force gauge reads the cartridge's recommended tracking force. Common ranges are 1.5–2.0g for most MM cartridges.

4. Verify with your digital scale placed at record height on the platter.

## Step 5: Set Anti-Skate

Anti-skate counteracts the inward force that pulls the tonearm toward the record center during playback (caused by groove friction). Correct anti-skate prevents the stylus from pressing harder against one groove wall.

A simple starting point: set anti-skate equal to your tracking force value. So if tracking force is 1.8g, set anti-skate to 1.8. Fine adjustment can be made by listening — properly set anti-skate produces equal channel balance and minimal distortion on inner grooves.

## Step 6: Set Tonearm Height (VTA)

Vertical tracking angle (VTA) determines whether the tonearm sits parallel to the record or angles slightly up or down at the pivot. The ideal VTA allows the stylus cantilever to sit at the manufacturer's specified angle (usually 20°) relative to the record surface.

A practical starting point: set the tonearm parallel to the record. If your tonearm allows VTA adjustment, listen on a few known records and experiment — raising the rear of the tonearm slightly tends to emphasize treble; lowering it softens it.

## Step 7: First Playback

Before playing your favorite record, start with something you're willing to risk — setup errors are most likely to cause tracking issues on the first play. Clean the record with your brush, clean the stylus with a proper stylus brush or stylus cleaning fluid (never drag your finger across a stylus), then lower the needle carefully.

Listen for channel balance, clarity in the inner grooves, and any evidence of mistracking (a buzzing or distorted quality). If you hear problems, revisit the steps above — alignment and tracking force are the most common culprits.
