Two thousand dollars is a meaningful investment — enough to build a system that will genuinely surprise you with what recorded music can do, and enough to avoid the compromises that make cheap audio feel disposable. It's also tight enough that every dollar needs to work hard.

This is a complete, carefully chosen system: an integrated amplifier, bookshelf speakers, a digital source for streaming, and a turntable for vinyl. If you don't want vinyl, the money saved goes toward better versions of the remaining components.

## The System

### Integrated Amplifier: ~$500

The integrated amplifier is the center of gravity. Every other component feeds into it.

For this budget, you want a solid-state integrated with enough power for typical bookshelf speakers (50–80 watts), a built-in phono stage, and clean, neutral amplification that doesn't impose its character on the music.

### Bookshelf Speakers: ~$500

Don't undersize the speaker budget. In any audio system, the speakers have the most influence on what you hear. At $500, the options are genuinely excellent.

### Streaming Source: ~$220

For digital music — streaming services, local files — a dedicated network streamer outperforms a phone or laptop in every audible way.

If budget allows, upgrade to the WiiM Pro Plus (~$219) for better internals and a built-in DAC.

### Turntable: ~$300

For vinyl, look for a direct-drive table with a built-in switchable phono preamp and a quality cartridge included. You want something you can set up correctly and trust to play records without damaging them — not a suitcase player, but a real deck with adjustable tracking force and anti-skate.

## What This System Does Well

This combination has been chosen for balance. No component is dramatically out of step with the others.

The Yamaha amplifier is a neutral, honest machine that will reveal what the speakers and sources are doing without editorializing. The ELAC speakers image precisely and play music with coherence across the frequency range. The WiiM streamer gives you the entire Tidal and Qobuz catalogs in lossless quality. The AT-LP120X is a turntable you can actually set up correctly and trust to play records without damaging them.

Together, they represent roughly what a serious audiophile would have considered a quality mid-range system fifteen years ago — which is to say, they are genuinely capable.

## What Comes Next

This system will not embarrass you when you upgrade later. The Yamaha will drive better speakers. The WiiM can be replaced by a better streamer feeding the same amplifier. The turntable cartridge can be upgraded (the AT-VM95 body accepts stylus upgrades up to the VM95SH Shibata).

The ELAC speakers are the first likely upgrade target if budget increases — moving to something like the KEF LS50 Meta opens up significantly more resolution and soundstage precision.

But as configured, this system plays music at a level that a casual listener would describe as revelatory. Which is exactly what it should do.
