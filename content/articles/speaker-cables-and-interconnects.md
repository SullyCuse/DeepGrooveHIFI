Cable discussion occupies a disproportionate amount of space in audio forums relative to the actual sonic impact cables have. On one extreme you have manufacturers charging thousands of dollars for wire with elaborate marketing about directionality and crystalline structure. On the other extreme you have engineers insisting cables can't possibly make any audible difference. The truth is considerably more grounded than either position.

## Speaker Cables: The Physics That Matter

A speaker cable has three electrical properties that affect its behavior: resistance, capacitance, and inductance.

**Resistance** is the most important and most misunderstood. Speaker cable resistance combines with your speaker's nominal impedance to form a voltage divider. A small portion of your amplifier's output is dropped across the cable rather than delivered to the speaker. The practical rule: keep speaker cable resistance below 5% of your speaker's nominal impedance.

For an 8-ohm speaker, that means total cable resistance (both conductors, both ends) below 0.4 ohms. At 16 AWG copper wire — a common starting point — resistance is approximately 0.013 ohms per foot. A 10-foot run (20 feet total wire length) has about 0.26 ohms of resistance. Well within limits.

For a 4-ohm speaker, the tolerance is half as generous. Longer runs with 4-ohm speakers benefit from heavier gauge wire (12 AWG or even 10 AWG) to maintain low resistance.

**The practical upshot:** for typical home use with 8-ohm speakers and runs under 15 feet, 16 AWG copper is genuinely sufficient. For runs over 20 feet or 4-ohm speakers, use 14 or 12 AWG. The law of diminishing returns applies quickly beyond 12 AWG for most home audio situations.

**Capacitance and inductance** matter primarily with very long runs or unusual amplifier designs. High capacitance in a speaker cable can destabilize some amplifiers, particularly older designs. High inductance causes a gentle high-frequency rolloff at extreme lengths. For typical home system cable runs, neither is a meaningful concern.

## What Doesn't Matter (Despite the Marketing)

**Oxygen-free copper (OFC)** is marginally purer than standard copper. At audio frequencies and typical cable lengths, the difference in conductivity is too small to measure, let alone hear. The marketing of OFC as a significant upgrade is exaggerated.

**Crystal structure, directional arrows, and break-in periods** have no established physical mechanism that would affect audio performance at the frequencies and power levels involved in home audio. These are marketing constructs.

**Exotic materials like silver** are measurably better conductors than copper — silver has about 5% lower resistivity. In a cable that already has very low resistance, a 5% improvement is audiophile-grade nothing.

## Interconnects: Signal-Level Cables

Interconnects carry line-level signals between components — from DAC to preamplifier, from streamer to integrated amplifier. The signal levels are much lower than speaker-level signals, and the cables have correspondingly different requirements.

**Shielding is the primary concern.** An interconnect cable picks up radio frequency interference and hum from nearby power cables and transformers. Quality shielding — a dense braid or foil around the signal conductors — prevents this. This is where spending money on interconnects actually returns something measurable: better-shielded cables in electrically noisy environments produce lower noise floors.

**Connector quality matters.** The mechanical contact between the RCA plug and the socket degrades with repeated connection and disconnection, and with oxidation over time. Quality connectors with proper plating (gold for corrosion resistance) and tight mechanical fit are worth paying for. They last longer and maintain better contact.

**Cable capacitance** affects interconnects more than speaker cables. High capacitance combined with a source component's output impedance creates a low-pass filter that rolls off high frequencies. For most modern components with low output impedances, this isn't a practical concern. For older high-impedance sources like some tube phono stages, it's worth considering.

## A Sensible Approach to Cable Buying

For **speaker cables**: buy by the foot from a bulk supplier (Blue Jeans Cable, Monoprice, Amazon Basics) at appropriate gauge for your run length. Spend the difference on music.

For **interconnects**: quality matters more here. A well-shielded cable with quality connectors from a reputable manufacturer in the $30–$80 range per pair is entirely appropriate for most systems. Beyond that price range, the engineering improvements become marginal.

The test to apply: if you can demonstrate an audible difference between cables in a properly controlled blind test with your own equipment, the difference is real for your system. If you can't, the difference exists only in the invoice.

## When Cables Actually Make a Difference

There are genuine situations where cable choice changes what you hear:

A ground loop introduced by an unshielded interconnect in a noisy electrical environment will hum audibly. Replacing it with a well-shielded cable fixes the problem. This is a real cable difference.

Speaker cable resistance with 4-ohm speakers on very long runs — 30 feet or more — will cause audible bass softening if you're using 18 AWG wire. Moving to 12 AWG solves it. This is also a real cable difference.

Marginal connector contact creating an intermittent or high-resistance connection will cause crackling, channel imbalance, or signal loss. Again, real and fixable.

These are engineering problems with engineering solutions. Pay for the engineering — proper gauge, quality shielding, solid connectors — and don't pay for the mythology.
