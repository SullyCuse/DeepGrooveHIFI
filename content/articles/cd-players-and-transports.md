The CD was declared dead so many times over the past two decades that it's easy to miss what's actually happening now: a quiet, genuine revival among serious listeners. CD sales have stabilized. Used CD prices for well-regarded pressings have climbed. Dedicated CD transport manufacturers have seen renewed demand. Something is happening here worth understanding.

## What a CD Player Actually Does

A CD player has two distinct functions: reading the disc and converting the digital data to analog. Understanding this separation helps clarify the choices available.

**The transport mechanism** spins the disc, reads data with a laser, performs error correction, and outputs a digital bitstream. Transport quality affects how accurately and cleanly the data is retrieved — and how much jitter (timing uncertainty) is present in that bitstream.

**The DAC stage** converts the digital stream to an analog signal. In a standalone CD player, these functions are combined. In a transport-plus-DAC setup, they're separated — the transport outputs a digital signal (typically coaxial SPDIF or AES/EBU) to an external DAC.

Most audiophile-focused CD setups today use a dedicated CD transport feeding a high-quality external DAC. This allows using the best available DAC technology — which continues to improve — with a transport mechanism optimized purely for disc reading.

## The Case for Physical Media in 2026

Several genuine arguments favor CD playback that streaming doesn't address.

**Mastering certainty.** When you buy a specific CD pressing, you know exactly which mastering you have. Streaming services host whatever version the label or distributor uploaded, which changes without notification and isn't always clearly labeled. For listeners who have identified specific masterings they prefer — a common situation with classic rock, jazz, and classical releases — physical media is the only reliable way to access that exact version.

**No infrastructure dependency.** A CD player works without internet connectivity, without a streaming subscription, without a server staying online. Your music library is a physical object that plays as long as the disc is readable and the player functions.

**Catalog reliability.** Streaming catalogs change. Albums are removed for licensing reasons, replaced with different versions, or made unavailable in specific regions. A physical library of 500 CDs is always those 500 albums.

**Sound quality at the bit level.** A 16-bit/44.1kHz CD is, by definition, bit-perfect lossless audio. It contains every bit of what was on the master at the time of pressing. A lossless streaming service delivers the same data — but only if the mastering and transfer are identical to what you have on CD, which they often aren't.

## Transport Quality: Does It Matter?

This is the most technically debated aspect of CD playback. Digital audio is supposed to be immune to transport quality — the bits are either right or wrong, and error correction handles the wrong ones. In theory, any CD transport producing bit-accurate output sounds identical.

In practice, jitter — timing errors in the digital output — affects the sound quality at the DAC. A higher-jitter transport produces a noisier, less stable clock signal that the DAC must work harder to recover. Modern DACs with asynchronous reclocking handle jitter well, but the DAC's reclocking circuitry has limits.

Premium transport mechanisms — from manufacturers like Pro-Ject, Audiolab, Jay's Audio, and Cambridge Audio — use higher-quality drive mechanisms, better vibration isolation, and lower-jitter output stages. Whether this is audible depends significantly on which DAC receives the signal.

The practical guidance: a mid-range CD transport feeding a DAC with good asynchronous USB or coaxial reclocking will outperform a poor transport feeding a minimal DAC. If your DAC has strong jitter rejection, transport quality matters less.

## What to Look For

**For a one-box CD player:** Cambridge Audio, Rega, Marantz, and Yamaha all offer respected integrated players at various price points. At the budget end, used players from Marantz and Denon from the 2010s offer excellent performance for minimal outlay.

**For a transport:** Pro-Ject's CD Box range, Audiolab's 6000CDT, and the Cambridge Audio CXC are consistently recommended entry points that output to an external DAC. Jay's Audio occupies the high-performance segment with purpose-built transport mechanisms.

**For used CD players:** The used market for high-quality players from Sony, Denon, Marantz, and Rotel is excellent value. These players were expensive when new and depreciated heavily with the shift to streaming. Many are sonically competitive with much more expensive current production gear. Verify the transport mechanism has been serviced or still operates reliably before buying.

## Ripping as an Alternative

Some audiophiles extract the best of both worlds by ripping their CD library to lossless files — typically FLAC — and playing the files from a local server through a dedicated streamer. This preserves the mastering certainty and catalog ownership of physical media while gaining the convenience of software-based playback.

A CD ripper running in EAC (Exact Audio Copy) or dBpoweramp with AccurateRip verification confirms bit-accurate extraction. The resulting files are genuinely identical to the CD data.

The decision between keeping a CD player in the chain or ripping and playing files depends on your workflow preferences. Both are legitimate approaches to getting the most from the physical software you own.

The CD isn't going anywhere. For listeners who care about which mastering they're hearing, it remains the most reliable format in common use.
